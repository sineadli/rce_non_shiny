Find an Instrument to Measure Your Outcome

To evaluate the effectiveness of an intervention you will have to decide how you will measure your outcome of interest.

This tool will help you identify an appropriate instrument for your evaluation. It provides a list of instruments that target a variety of outcomes and links to other resources that can help your find an instrument.

As you search for an instrument, it is important to keep a few key ideas in mind[MM1] [SB2] [KP3] .

Does tThe instrument aligns with your outcome of interest?.

The instrument you use should reflect your outcome of interest. This tool identifies relevant topic areas for each instrument (i.e., communication, social-emotional learning, or parental engagement), but it is important to review the measures yourself and ensure they capture your specific outcome.

Is tThe instrument is designed for your target population?.

If your outcome of interest is student stress, and you find a survey intended to measure stress, you want to make sure the instrument is designed for students and not adults. Similarly, an instrument designed for high school students may not be appropriate for elementary school students and an instrument designed in 1995 to measure technology literacy may not be appropriate today.

Has tThe instrument has been validated?.

Validity refers to the extent to which an instrument measures what it is supposed to measure. Many of the instruments below have been validated by external researchers. This is not the only consideration when selecting an instrument, but all else equal, it is better to use a validated instrument.

 

Instrument List

Below is a list of instruments that cover a variety of topics. Instruments can be multidimensional, which means sometimes only certain sections or items will be appropriate depending on what outcome you are interested in.

Many of the instruments listed below are surveys. See our how to use a survey instrument guide for tips on working with surveys[MM4] [SB5] .
