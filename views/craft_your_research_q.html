Craft your research question

Your research question will drive the rest of your study. A good question has four building blocks, which we call the "A, B, C, D's". Click on sections A through D below to see examples, read a description, and build your own question, piece by piece.

The blue print: Does a do b among c compared to d?

Example: Does DreamBox Learning increase student achievement on the Smarter Balanced math assessment among 5th grade English Learners compared to similar students with no access to Dreambox Learning?

Example

  • Increase student achievement on the PARCC reading interim assessment
  • Decrease chronic absenteeism

C. Among whom are you hoping to see results?

What is the group of individuals you are trying to impact? This could be the same as the individuals using the technology, or it could be different. For example, students may be using the technology to increase their own achievement or teachers could be using the technology to increase their students' achievement. In both cases, the answer to Part C would be: students.

Example

  • 6th grade students
  • 5th grade English learners
  • 3rd through 5th grade teachers

D. Who are you comparing them against?

It is important to be clear about whether you are comparing users to a group with no access to an educational technology, or with access to a different educational technology. As shown above, this will determine how you interpret your findings later on and what kinds of decisions this data will empower you to make.

Example

  • Similar 5th grade English Learners with no access to DreamBox Learning
  • Similar 5th grade English Learners that use IXL Math instead of DreamBox Learning
